Whenever fights with mass appeal came along, they brought in a wave of people who maybe never watched fights, used to watch fights but fell off for whatever reason, or were casual fans. Personally, I think the MMA community used the term “casual fan” far too much because there were a lot of fight fans who watched a ton of fights and still had consistently terrible takes.

Sometimes those kinds of fans leaned more toward the betting angle of the fights, and sometimes they didn’t. I think of a casual fan as someone who tuned in for the big fights with a recognizable name. In the UFC in 2026, that could be more difficult to identify because there were fewer fighters with mass appeal like Chuck Liddell, Tito Ortiz, Anderson Silva, Chael Sonnen, GSP, Brock Lesnar, Forrest Griffin, Stephan Bonnar, Conor McGregor, and yes, Ronda Rousey. COVID could also get a special shout-out.

What I’m getting at is that when those waves of new viewers tuned in for something big, you had to brace yourself for some really low-IQ takes. It happened every single time, and Rousey vs. Carano was no different.

I think a lot of people will understand this, but I know for a fact many of you don’t: there were tons of people who just didn’t really know anything about the UFC. It has gotten better over time, but there were still people who needed clarity when someone said “UFC.” “Oh, is that the fighting thing? They fight in a cage?”

A lot of people didn’t know who Dana White was, but they might have known Ronda Rousey. They may have seen some highlights here and there, but especially today, social media can be so specifically curated that you can craft exactly what you want to watch and have it fed to you all the time. So it didn’t surprise me at all if someone didn’t know jack shit about this sport.

I also think Dana White being so heavily involved with Trump piqued some curiosity about who he was and what the UFC was for people who had literally never paid any attention to this sport.

What was unique about Ronda Rousey vs. Gina Carano was that there was an audience familiar with Ronda Rousey. She cast a very wide net of fans, many of whom were loyal to her. The hardcores like me were going to watch her fight because we watch every fucking fight, and a Netflix card was interesting.

Ronda had a fan base that tuned in to watch her, and after she retired, they stopped watching too. So those people came back, and they probably watched because it was on fucking Netflix. Had this been a PPV, I don’t think it would have done well. Maybe it would have done decently for a PPV in 2026, but nowhere near the numbers they got by showing it on Netflix.

Let’s also keep in mind that many people who were even kind of familiar with Ronda Rousey may have only ever watched fights at a sports bar while they were inebriated and with a friend who watched fights, or with an ex-significant other, but never got familiar with the UFC product during that time.

Besides, if you tuned into a Ronda Rousey fight, it only lasted so long. Her fights rarely went past round one. I’d also like to point out that a lot of people tuned in for Rousey vs. Holm and Rousey vs. Nunes. They heard about this judoka bad bitch mowing through everyone, and then she got knocked out. Twice in a row.

I should also mention that maybe there were some fans who only knew Ronda from WWE, but that could not have been too significant a number.

As far as Gina Carano, enough time had passed that not many people really knew who Gina Carano was. Not the fighting Gina Carano, anyway. And those who did, I mean, come on, guys, it was a flash in the pan. It only lasted a couple of years, if that, but it happened during a time when MMA was gaining tons of momentum: 2008 and 2009.

The rest knew her from The Mandalorian. But she was attractive, so that made up for a lot. It was part of her appeal in 2008, and it was still part of the appeal now.

So Netflix, Ronda Rousey, Gina Carano, and yes, even Jake Paul, opened the floodgates for fair-weather fans, casual fans, and new viewers. A majority of them thought, or at least may have thought, they were watching the UFC. They did not have product familiarity because they just didn’t watch. I know, shocking for some of you, but it is a fact. I can tell you this with 100% certainty.

It didn’t matter that Bruce Buffer wasn’t announcing, that Joe Rogan wasn’t there, or that there was a black canvas and a WWE-style LCD screen tunnel. A lot more people who don’t watch MMA are simply more familiar with professional wrestling than MMA. Add a gimmicky-looking LCD walkout entrance to the mix, and even subconsciously, it felt a bit funky.

Ronda Rousey vs. Gina Carano lasted 17 seconds. Ronda didn’t even throw Gina to the canvas. She hit a double-leg, got the armbar, and it was done.

Guess who wasn’t surprised? People like you and me.

The people who were surprised, or maybe even skeptical, were not people like you and me. The hardcore and regular MMA fans were not surprised by this. Everyone else was probably skeptical, confused, or maybe thought it was outright fake and moved on.

You know what else I didn’t stop to consider? Did people even know why these two were fighting? Sure, there were countdown videos and stuff, but the broadcast didn’t do the greatest job of explaining it, and even then, it didn’t really make sense.

They had both been gone a long time, and now they wanted to fight each other. A fight that could have happened but never did, and now here it was? Well, I suppose that kind of made sense.

What I found kind of funny was the hardcore fans and media saying, “I told you so,” when the fight ended quickly and early. It’s like, who were you flexing to? Anyone who followed you already knew this.

I left some room for pageantry. Maybe Ronda would want to prove something about her stand-up. Maybe she would make it a touch more dramatic. Maybe. But since she didn’t, and since it was all strictly business, that showed exactly why this fight was not fixed. That is exactly how it would have gone down every time if Ronda executed a no-bullshit approach to the fight.

A fix is predetermined. It is secretly arranged. Someone agrees to something. Some say “rigged,” but these two were not under any obligation to the promotion. No one was forced into or pigeonholed into taking this fight. That was kind of the whole thing about this event. MVP was presenting itself as a place where fighters were treated fairly.

Ronda Rousey was highly favored, and the outcome was highly predictable. If this fight had been rehearsed, it would have looked ridiculous. You’ve seen these two act. It does not look good, and that is with a script and months, if not years, of production.

If you think this fight was fixed, I actually understand the circumstances that would lead you to that conclusion, but it would not have looked that clean.

Earlier I asked: why were these two fighting? I know why. Sure, part of it was that it was a fight people did talk about. When Ronda was coming up, and even at her peak, Ronda vs. Gina was mentioned from time to time. It strikes me as strange that some people who claim to be tenured fans say that it wasn’t. It was.

Now, the hype and anticipation around it were basically nonexistent because Gina would never commit. Sometimes you’d hear about talks with Gina and Dana, but it never really culminated in anything. It was also the general consensus, again, for fans like me and those who actively watched, that Rousey would decimate Gina Carano.

Gina was doing movie shit. She used the attention from fighting to move on to other things. There was no reason to go backward. We wanted to see Rousey vs. Cyborg. Even Gina fought Cyborg. That was the fight we wanted, and that was the one being talked about.

Why this fight took place and made sense for both Ronda Rousey and Gina Carano is what I call image laundering. That was the primary reason.

Sure, Ronda fought her hero. Gina lost weight and got healthy. It was a fight they both wanted. All of that is cute and true, but in my opinion, image laundering was first, money was second, and everything else was not even close. It’s what I thought before, and it’s what I think now.

Ronda Rousey’s career did not end the way she wanted it to, and her relationship with the media and fans tanked because of how she handled her losses. Anyone who was around for the buildup to Ronda vs. Amanda Nunes remembers this too.

So Ronda came back against someone with a name and a pioneer in Gina Carano, and she got to leave on a win. She got to remind everyone who she was, or who she thought she was, at the very least. She got one last taste of fame, and the fans did too. Absence makes the heart grow fonder, all that shit.

Now she is arguably in a better spot to do something else, whether it’s another fight, promoting, making comic books, or whatever, or to go back into retirement. She is now in a better position than she was before.

Gina Carano was the same exact thing. I’m not going to get into the politics of what Gina Carano said that got her canceled. For someone who wants to tell stories, as she puts it, she really went about trying to make a point or a statement in the dumbest way possible by essentially retweeting or posting someone else’s viewpoint that she agreed with.

Then later on, she used Star Wars memes or pictures to further drive her viewpoint and opinion on hot-button social and political topics at the time. And it’s like, why? Was there no one to advise you or have your best interests in mind? Was there not a more creative, better, smarter, more lucrative approach to saying what you wanted to say, Gina? Instead of shitposting? It was really quite remarkable.

So she came back after a settlement with Disney, and essentially, what I think this was about was vindication.

Look, I may have been beaten down by the system, but I am not broken. I am stronger than I’ve ever been. I stand by what I said and who I am, and to prove that, I’ll get into the cage with one of the all-time greatest to ever do it in women’s MMA, stand before the media, and stake my claim.

And by the way, if anyone is listening, umm, I’d really like to act again or maybe even direct. Wink wink, Netflix. Wink wink.

So that’s what they did. Gina Carano was not going to win this fight unless Ronda Rousey made a mistake by trying to compensate for something or by putting on too many theatrics. Gina Carano spent a very long time just getting into shape.

Fighters who actively fight and who are in shape will tell you there is a difference between being in shape and being in fight shape. There is no way Gina was in fight shape. I think Gina came in with a game plan. I think she trained for all the scenarios. She was at a great camp. I truly believe she did wake up at 3 a.m. and think of all the ways the fight could go, and that she prepared for those things.

But so did everyone else who fought Ronda. And those were active fighters.

A couple of last things now that this event is over: I enjoyed the card overall. It reminded me of a Strikeforce-Bellator hybrid. The production was good as far as the in-arena look.

I respect Mauro Ranallo and what he does, but ever since he started doing Jake Paul events from the beginning, I just haven’t been a huge fan of his style. What would you call it? Figures of speech, hyperbole, idiomatic comparisons. Like, “he tapped faster than a credit card,” or “folded like a lawn chair.” I get why he uses them, and I admire the creativity, but the older he gets, the dumber they sound, and it is just constant.

I know that is a personal opinion, and maybe that works for some people and for the masses, but I think I strive for a much more minimal and subdued approach to commentary these days. I want dead air from the commentary booth more often so that void can be filled with the sound of the crowd, and even more so the sound of feet on canvas, the punches, and the cage.

I love a two-man booth, but Mauro can really count for two people.

The pacing sucked, so we got too much Ariel Helwani, and fighters are so terrible at talking anyway that the last thing I want is more of that post-fight. Too much Ariel everywhere. If he’s going to be involved, I wish it were more in the storytelling aspect leading up to a fight because Netflix-MVP dropped the ball on that.

Their promo packages and explanations of who these fighters are and what they’ve done were terrible. Granted, they were not going to get clearance to use any UFC-owned media, but still. Tell a better story, especially with the amount of time between fights.

I enjoyed the prelims. Everyone was right about the squash matches, but nobody talked about David Mgoyan vs. Albert Morales, which I thought was really good matchmaking. It was a challenging fight for David, but not one he couldn’t win. A tough but passable test for a prospect.

It should have been talked about more, like Jason Jackson, the Phumi Adriano fight, or Namo and Jake. Fazil looked good. Parnasse was fun. You all know the talking points of this card at this stage. I loved the black canvas. Nate’s blood looked awesome, almost cartoonish, on the black canvas.

I was not a fan of calling it the hexagon. Just call it a cage. I get why they did it, but it sounded too diet-UFC to me. I didn’t mind the hexagon itself because if they used an octagon, then broadcasters were going to say “octagon,” and then they would have to pay the UFC for infringing on the trademark.

I guess I’m looking for a promotion that does better storytelling and finds more unique, creative ways to tell those stories. Less bells and whistles. Less fabricated beef and all that garbage. Make it look cool and keep it minimal.

They can do way more with a more focused and impactful approach because they won’t put on as many events as the UFC. And less Jake Paul, too. You let him talk too much, and he sounds stupid as fuck.

Anyway, decent overall. Looking forward to more.
