UFC 324: GAETHJE VS. PIMBLETT. The Paramount era begins...  
It could also be called "The Advertisement Era"

<br><br>

I paid around $100 for the premium annual subscription for Paramount+. My girlfriend asked me, "Are there going to be ads?" I laughed. I didn’t even need to read the fine print to know what was coming. I went for the higher tier because I don’t want to miss out on anything UFC-related that might end up behind a paywall. But I was also pretty certain they were going to run advertisements during the live broadcast no matter what tier I paid for. And guess what?

<br><br>

A&nbsp;D&nbsp;V&nbsp;E&nbsp;R&nbsp;T&nbsp;I&nbsp;S&nbsp;E&nbsp;M&nbsp;E&nbsp;N&nbsp;T&nbsp;S&nbsp;&nbsp;E&nbsp;V&nbsp;E&nbsp;R&nbsp;Y&nbsp;W&nbsp;H&nbsp;E&nbsp;R&nbsp;E

<br><br>

Ever since the Fertittas sold the UFC, there’s been a sharper focus on monetizing every inch of the product. The usual stuff has been there forever: in-octagon ads, commentary reads, Buffer reads, and standard commercials between fights. What’s changed is the density. Now it’s everything—your name on the canvas, the UFC prep point sponsored, sponsorship stickers on the commentators’ headsets, walkouts getting replaced by ads, advertisements between rounds. Even when they *do* show the fighters in the corners, an L-frame ad pops up and you can’t hear the corner audio anyway.

<br><br>

During this event, Ghostface from *Scream* was shown walking around behind fighters (Waldo Acosta specifically), and that rolled right into a trailer for *Scream*. It’s a massive amount of ads, and I don’t see that changing.

<br><br>

To me this was expected—maybe not across most of the main card like they did, but still. The amount of money Paramount paid for this partnership was astronomical, so of course they’re going to monetize everything. Dana White was asked about it and said he’ll look into it, and that he’s sure things will change, but that’s not really up to Dana White. If anything changes, I think it’ll be *how* they present the ads, not how many they run. Instead of cutting away and making you miss things, they’ll try to show ads side-by-side with what you actually want to see. But if there’s a fight between the audio—walkout/corner audio vs. ad audio—my guess is the advertising wins.

<br><br>

This is why I’m calling it the advertisement era. We’re about to see how advertising is approached, evolves, and gets implemented into live sports without feeling completely intrusive. It’s gonna feel like the Truman Show.

<br><br>

As I’m writing this, I’m seeing that ads were trending on Twitter too. It reads: "FANS SLAM ADS DURING UFC 324 DEBUT ON PARAMOUNT+"

<br><br>

Listen, MMA fans (really UFC or betting fans) are ultra shitty in general. They’re like 87% fairweather fans, and I would truly believe that statistic even though I have zero data. But their complaints about ads are justified, and I only saw a couple of bootlickers say they didn’t mind.

<br><br>

OK — so TONS of advertising. The next takeaway I had was "BLINK-182".

<br><br>

Travis Barker’s involvement with MMA goes back to his fandom, but also the business side of things—Famous Stars and Straps sponsored fighters back in the day. Anyways, Travis and Blink-182 were featured heavily: their music, Travis interviews, Travis “playing” drums in the octagon (not live, but in a video), and him in the crowd with Kourtney... it was a lot. Too much.

<br><br>

There was a lot of filler at the beginning of the broadcast too because quite a few fights fell out, so I’m sure they were prepping for a longer run time that was supposed to be filled with fighting, not talking. Michael Johnson vs. Alexander Hernandez, Cam Smotherman, Harrison vs. Nunes. This was less of an issue for me, but it definitely pissed a lot of the fairweather fans off. Then again... they’ll complain about literally everything, including a good fight.

<br><br>

Dominick Cruz was inducted into the Hall of Fame, which is very much deserved, and he was one of my favorite fighters when he was active... well, as active as he could be given the injuries. But he is still a style I like to study.

<br><br>

When the main card started, they did a really good promo package to kick off the new era.

7 right, 4 wrong. My K/D ratio is 1.75.

<br><br>

At the very last second I changed my pick from Natalia Silva to Rose Namajunas, and that happens to be the fight I disagreed with the scorecards on. I think Rose won the first two rounds and Natalia obviously took the third. I also see an argument for Andrey Pulyaev defeating Ateba Gautier, but I’d need to rewatch it. My dog was going insane and I missed chunks of the fight.

<br><br>

I enjoyed Ty Miller’s clean, straight punches. He had some difficulty against a southpaw in Adam Fugitt, but he did well. Adam performed well given my expectations, and I thought he had excellent kicks to the body.

<br><br>

Josh Hokit is a better fighter and a much better athlete than Denzel Freeman. Freeman had nothing—covering up and barely countering the onslaught. I think he may have landed a couple shots, but he didn’t keep that going. Hokit’s power wasn’t impressive in this fight, but as he gets more comfortable standing, he’ll be able to see openings better. Right now, it’s all pressure and volume. He’ll need composure eventually, otherwise he’ll get sniped.

<br><br>

I still despise the guy’s absolutely stupid WWE gimmick, which is just him impersonating wrestlers and Chael Sonnen... verbatim, might I add. His only original thing is saying one controversial thing. It’s stupid.

<br><br>

Moving on to Arnold Allen. I really thought Arnold would win. He had some real crispy striking and some good movement, but eventually Jean’s power fucked him up, and Arnold also gassed out. Jean goes on and I don’t. He’s a bit of a donk—insane, maybe acoustic, emotional—but he’s fun to watch. If someone tried to do absurdist, stereotypical comedy on a Brazilian MMA fighter, you’d come up with Jean Silva.

<br><br>

Derrick Lewis and Waldo were a letdown. Waldo just waited and capitalized on Derrick making mistakes. Derrick didn’t want to make too many mistakes, but he did... Waldo is good, not great. He moves OK. He’s a different name in the heavyweight division and will continue to be in interesting matchups. I need to rewatch more of his fights because I haven’t come to a conclusion on how I feel about him.

<br><br>

Sean O’Malley wins a decision. Some are saying Song Yadong was robbed. I disagree. I could see it being a controversial decision—like it could go either way—but I felt Sean won. It comes down to the first round. Sean landed the cleaner, better strikes at a higher volume in round 1. Stats back that up. I’m not big on looking at stats and making a decision, but in this instance it totally makes sense. You look at the first round, you see O’Malley landing at a higher clip, and you see the damage on Song’s face. What sways people is Song’s explosivity and power, but one or two big moments can’t eclipse who did the real cumulative damage. Sean wasn’t super hurt by the end of that round and neither was Song. But Song had visible damage. The numbers don’t lie on this one.

<br><br>

Justin Gaethje and Paddy Pimblett. This was a tough one to pick for me personally for a few reasons:

<br><br>

1. Both fighters get hit often.  
2. Justin Gaethje is 37 in the lightweight division.  
3. Paddy is younger but, skill-wise, not the better fighter.  
4. ...It’s Gaethje. He could be the best fighter on planet earth and still lose cuz he is chaos.

<br><br>

Ultimately, I went with Gaethje because I thought he’d have better cardio over five rounds, he is the better fighter, and I didn’t think he’d get taken down. There’s the pressure of the moment, the aura, and the possibility that Gaethje is just gonna look old.

<br><br>

The only way I can describe this fight is that these two BEAT THE SHIT OUT OF EACH OTHER. People want to complain about the eye poke that Gaethje did and nut shots... Even if those didn’t happen, Paddy was going to get fucked up regardless. They both really beat the shit out of each other, but Gaethje definitely put it on Paddy more. They both left something in the cage—especially Paddy.

<br><br>

Gaethje did well to try to exhaust Paddy as much as he could. Those early rounds, Gaethje didn’t do too much in terms of volume. He just let Paddy throw bombs. But kudos to Paddy, who didn’t slow down as much as I thought he would, and the dude can really take a punch. I don’t know how his chin will fare after this fight, but let me just tell you that he has a fucking chin. He took 25 minutes worth of absolute bombs.

<br><br>

Although an entertaining fight... I don’t see either of them beating Arman or Ilia. It was an entertaining main event in terms of action, but it misses the mark of the true best in the world and what was at stake. It was an interim title... that’s why I scored it a 9/10. I think it would be more fair to put an 8/10... we need better storyline, stakes, and skill level for the 9 and 10 marks.

<br><br>

Nonetheless. About 60% of the card was a good time.
